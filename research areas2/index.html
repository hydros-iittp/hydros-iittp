  • Hydrologic Monitoring and Prediction

    The real-time monitoring of water resources and extreme hydrologic events is highly underscored for a country like ours. Droughts and floods occur on a frequent basis and a lack of monitoring system often results in poor management of water resources and delayed decision making and planning.

    Understanding of hydrological sensitivity to both natural and human induced changes from watershed to global scales is essential for sustainability of water resources. Climate and land use/land cover changes are two major drivers that can alter water quantity and quality. Warming temperatures driven by climate change can intensify hydrologic processes, while land cover changes driven by agricultural intensification and urbanization can influence hydrologic extremes and water quality. Our research group has been actively working on to understand the impacts of climate, land cover/land use, and other anthropogenic activities (irrigation and water regulation) on water availability and on the hydrologic cycle. For instance, in one of our studies, we found that significant warming and decline in the monsoon season precipitation has resulted in declined evapotranspiration (ET) and streamflow in the Ganga river basin. In another study, we reported that water availability in the Ganga river basin is more sensitive to projected changes in the monsoon season precipitation rather than air temperature. Currently, we are working on to evaluate the role of irrigation and reservoirs on water cycle in the Indian sub-continental river basins. Our research focuses on understanding the role of climate variability on the surface and groundwater systems in India. Understanding this linkage not only will provide us insights about the water resources management but also will be the key aspect of the future water sustainability in India. Our recent paper (Nature Geoscience) highlights the relative contribution of pumping and the variability in the summer monsoon on groundwater storage change in India. In the follow-up work, we have established a key linkage between groundwater recharge and rainfall intensity in India, which will provide important insights to the policy makers for enhancing the groundwater recharge in India. We intend to answer the questions: How do climate and land use/land cover changes affect water quality and quantity at watershed to regional scales? How will land cover changes driven by agricultural intensification and urbanization under changing climatic conditions pose challenges for sustainability of natural resources? How can observations from in-situ measurements and present era remote sensing satellites, integrated with medium/high resolutions land surface hydrologic models (e.g. VIC and SWAT), help to identify highly sensitive/vulnerable areas experiencing changes in water quality and quantity?
